Electrical Code Compliance Resources

Understanding electrical codes is essential for ensuring your home's safety, meeting insurance requirements, and avoiding costly corrections during inspections.

Fairfax County Codes Permit Guide

Why Electrical Code Compliance Matters

Electrical codes aren't just bureaucratic red tape—they're essential safety standards developed based on years of research, testing, and real-world incidents. At AJ Long Electric, with over 1,200 5-star reviews, we're committed to helping homeowners understand these codes and ensuring all our work exceeds compliance standards.

Safety

Electrical codes are designed to prevent fires, electrical shocks, and other hazards. Code-compliant installations help protect your family and property from preventable dangers.

Insurance Coverage

Many insurance policies require electrical work to be code-compliant and performed by licensed professionals. Non-compliant work could void your coverage in case of electrical-related incidents.

Property Value

Code-compliant electrical systems enhance your home's value and prevent issues during home inspections when selling. Documented permits show potential buyers that work was done properly.

Important Note

The information provided here is for general guidance and educational purposes only. Electrical codes can change, and local amendments may apply. Always consult with a licensed electrician or your local building department for the most current and specific code requirements for your project.

Fairfax County Electrical Codes

Fairfax County follows the Virginia Uniform Statewide Building Code (USBC), which adopts the National Electrical Code (NEC) with some local amendments.

Current Electrical Code in Fairfax County

As of 2025, Fairfax County follows the 2021 Virginia Residential Code and the 2020 National Electrical Code (NEC) with Virginia amendments. The code is updated periodically, so it's important to check with the county for the most current version before starting any electrical project.

Common Residential Electrical Code Requirements

GFCI Protection Requirements

Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) protection is required in areas where water may be present. In Fairfax County, GFCI protection is required for receptacles in the following locations:

  • Bathrooms
  • Kitchens (receptacles serving countertops)
  • Outdoor areas
  • Crawl spaces and unfinished basements
  • Garages and accessory buildings
  • Receptacles within 6 feet of laundry, utility, and wet bar sinks
  • Boathouses

AFCI Protection Requirements

AFCI requirements vary by jurisdiction and enforcement practices. Contact local authorities for specific requirements.

Receptacle Spacing Requirements

Receptacles must be placed to ensure that no point along the floor line of any wall space is more than 6 feet from a receptacle. Additional requirements include:

  • Receptacles must be installed in any wall space 2 feet or wider
  • Kitchen countertops: Receptacles must be placed so that no point along the wall line is more than 24 inches from a receptacle
  • Island and peninsula countertops: At least one receptacle is required if the space is 12 inches × 24 inches or larger
  • Bathroom: At least one receptacle must be installed within 3 feet of the outside edge of each basin
  • Outdoor: At least one receptacle must be installed at the front and back of the dwelling

Electrical Panel Requirements

Electrical panels must meet specific placement and clearance requirements:

  • Working space: Minimum 30 inches wide × 36 inches deep × 78 inches high clear space in front of the panel
  • Panel height: The center of the grip of the highest overcurrent device cannot be more than 6 feet 7 inches above the floor
  • Prohibited locations: Panels cannot be installed in bathrooms, clothes closets, or where subject to physical damage
  • Dedicated space: No pipes or ducts can be installed above the panel
  • Lighting: Sufficient lighting must be provided at the panel location

Lighting Requirements

The code specifies minimum lighting requirements for residential dwellings:

  • At least one wall switch-controlled lighting outlet in every habitable room, bathroom, hallway, stairway, attached garage, and outdoor entrance
  • Bathrooms: At least one lighting fixture must be installed
  • Stairways: At least one light fixture controlled by switches at each floor level for stairways with six or more steps
  • Storage/utility spaces: At least one lighting outlet must be installed in attics, under-floor spaces, and utility spaces used for equipment requiring servicing
  • Energy efficiency: Lighting efficiency requirements may vary and should be checked with the current energy code

Electric Vehicle Charging Requirements

As of 2025, Fairfax County has specific requirements for electric vehicle charging installations:

  • Level 2 EV chargers (240V) require an electrical permit
  • Dedicated circuit required: Typically 40-60 amp circuit depending on the charger specifications
  • GFCI protection: Required for all EV charging outlets
  • Disconnect means: A readily accessible disconnecting means must be installed
  • Outdoor installations: Must use weather-resistant equipment and proper mounting heights
  • Load calculations: For new construction, EV charger loads must be included in service calculations

For new construction, Fairfax County encourages "EV-ready" garages with appropriate electrical capacity and conduit to simplify future EV charger installation.

Energy Efficiency Codes

In addition to safety requirements, Fairfax County also enforces energy efficiency codes that affect electrical installations:

  • Lighting efficiency: At least 90% of permanently installed lighting fixtures must use high-efficacy lamps (typically LED)
  • Controls: Various lighting control requirements may apply, such as dimmer switches, occupancy sensors, or timers in certain locations
  • HVAC: Electrical requirements for heating and cooling equipment must meet minimum efficiency standards
  • Circuit identification: Separate metering and circuits may be required for monitoring energy usage

Permit & Inspection Guide

Understanding when permits are required and how to navigate the inspection process in Fairfax County.

When Electrical Permits Are Required

In Fairfax County, electrical permits are generally required for new installations, alterations, or replacements of electrical systems. Here's a quick guide:

Permit Required

  • Electrical panel replacements and upgrades
  • Meter replacements
  • Certain service cable replacement jobs
  • EV charger installations
  • Generator installations
  • Solar panel installations
  • Swimming pool wiring

Permit Not Required (Generally)

  • Replacing existing outlets, switches, or light fixtures in the same location
  • Minor repairs to existing wiring
  • Low-voltage wiring (thermostats, doorbells, security systems)
  • Replacing fuses or circuit breakers of the same rating

Always confirm with Fairfax County if you're unsure whether your project requires a permit.

The Permit Process

Navigating the permit process in Fairfax County involves several steps:

1

Application Submission

Fairfax County offers both online and in-person permit applications. You'll need to provide:

  • Property information
  • Description of work
  • Contractor information (if applicable)
  • Basic electrical plans for more complex projects
  • Permit fees payment
2

Plan Review (if needed)

For more complex projects, a plan review may be required. Simple electrical permits like replacement services often don't require detailed plan reviews and may be issued on the spot. For larger projects, plan reviews typically take 1-3 weeks, depending on complexity and current county workload.

3

Permit Issuance

Once your application is approved, you'll receive your permit, which must be displayed at the work site. The permit will include details about required inspections. Most electrical permits in Fairfax County are valid for 6 months from the date of issuance, with the possibility of extensions if requested.

4

Perform Work

Complete the electrical work according to the approved plans and in compliance with all applicable codes. Keep the work accessible for inspection until the final approval is granted.

5

Schedule Inspections

Schedule required inspections online or by phone. Fairfax County typically requires inspections at various stages of the project, including rough-in inspections (before walls are closed) and final inspections when the work is complete.

6

Final Approval

Once all inspections are passed, you'll receive final approval. Keep records of all permits and approvals for future reference, as they may be important when selling your home or for insurance purposes.

Inspection Checklist

Preparing for electrical inspections can help ensure your project passes the first time. Here's what inspectors typically look for:

Rough-In Inspection (before walls are closed)

  • Proper wire sizing for the intended load
  • Correct wiring methods and materials
  • Proper support and protection of cables
  • Box fill calculations (not too many wires in a box)
  • Proper grounding and bonding
  • Required clearances and spacing
  • Protection of wires from damage (nail plates where needed)

Final Inspection (after completion)

  • All devices and fixtures properly installed
  • GFCI and AFCI protection where required
  • Proper panel labeling
  • Smoke and carbon monoxide detectors (if part of the permit)
  • Required clearances around electrical equipment
  • Weatherproof covers for outdoor installations
  • Tamper-resistant receptacles where required
  • Proper operation of all installed equipment

Service Inspection (for panel upgrades/replacements)

  • Service entrance conductor sizing
  • Proper grounding and bonding
  • Correct panel installation height and clearances
  • Proper service disconnect
  • Weather-tight connections for outdoor components
  • Surge protection (if required)
  • Proper labeling of circuits

Common Reasons for Failed Inspections:

  • Improper wire sizing or type
  • Missing GFCI or AFCI protection
  • Inadequate grounding
  • Overfilled electrical boxes
  • Insufficient working clearance around electrical panels
  • Improper support for cables
  • Missing permits for work performed

Common Code Violations

Learn about the most frequently encountered electrical code violations and how to avoid them.

Overloaded Electrical Panels

Adding too many circuits to an electrical panel or using double-tapped breakers (two wires on one breaker) when not designed for it.

Why it's dangerous:

Overloaded panels can cause overheating, breaker failure, and increased fire risk.

Missing GFCI Protection

Outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, and outdoor areas lacking required GFCI protection.

Why it's dangerous:

Increases risk of electrical shock in areas where water may be present.

Improper Wire Connections

Using incorrect wire nuts, loose connections, or electrical tape instead of proper connectors.

Why it's dangerous:

Poor connections can create heat, arcing, and fire hazards.

Insufficient Panel Clearance

Not maintaining the required 30" wide × 36" deep × 78" high working space in front of electrical panels.

Why it's dangerous:

Insufficient clearance restricts access during emergencies and maintenance.

Incorrect Wire Sizing

Using undersized wires for the circuit amperage or distance (voltage drop).

Why it's dangerous:

Undersized wires can overheat, damaging insulation and causing fires.

Missing Junction Box Covers

Junction boxes left open or wire splices made outside of junction boxes.

Why it's dangerous:

Exposed connections increase shock hazard and can spread fire if arcing occurs.

Homeowner Tips for Code Compliance

Hire licensed professionals: Licensed electricians are familiar with code requirements and can ensure compliant installations.

Get permits when required: Even if it seems like extra work, permits help ensure your electrical work is safe and up to code.

Don't cut corners: Using proper materials and methods may cost a bit more initially but saves money and ensures safety in the long run.

Understand the "why": Electrical codes aren't arbitrary—they're developed in response to real safety issues and fire hazards.

Document everything: Keep records of all permits, inspections, and electrical work for future reference and when selling your home.
