Electrical Panel Issues

The most common and serious electrical issues found during home inspections are related to the electrical panel.

Double-Tapped Breakers

Multiple wires connected to a single breaker terminal designed for only one wire. This creates loose connections and potential fire hazards.

Fix: Install additional breakers or use approved tandem breakers, ensuring one wire per terminal.

Federal Pacific Panels

Federal Pacific Electric panels with Stab-Lok breakers have a known history of failing to trip during overloads, creating serious fire risks.

Fix: Complete panel replacement is the only safe solution. No repairs are acceptable.

Zinsco or Sylvania Panels

These panels have a design flaw where breakers can appear to be off while still allowing current to flow, creating fire risks.

Fix: Complete replacement with a modern, approved electrical panel.

Missing Knockouts

Open holes in the electrical panel allow pests to enter and potentially cause short circuits, while also creating shock hazards.

Fix: Install proper knockout seals or blanking plates to cover all openings.

Overfused Circuits

Breakers or fuses with higher amperage than the connected wire gauge can handle, causing wires to overheat before the breaker trips.

Fix: Replace with correctly sized breakers matching wire capacity or rewire with appropriate gauge wire.

Inadequate Panel Capacity

Older 60-100 amp panels that can't safely handle modern electrical demands, leading to frequent breaker trips and potential overheating.

Fix: Upgrade to a 200 amp panel to meet modern electrical demands.

Outlet & Switch Issues

Common electrical violations with outlets and switches that home inspectors frequently flag.

Missing GFCI Protection

Standard outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoors, and basements that should have GFCI protection against shock hazards.

Fix: Install GFCI outlets or GFCI circuit breakers to provide proper protection in these areas.

Ungrounded Outlets

Three-prong outlets that aren't properly grounded, creating potential shock hazards and damaging sensitive electronics.

Fix: Install proper grounding or replace with GFCI outlets (with "No Equipment Ground" labels).

Painted-Over Outlets

Outlets with paint covering the plug slots can create poor connections, overheating, and prevent proper plug insertion.

Fix: Replace painted outlets with new ones (never attempt to clean paint off).

Missing Outlet Covers

Exposed wiring from missing outlet or switch cover plates presents a serious shock hazard, especially for children.

Fix: Install proper cover plates on all outlets, switches, and junction boxes.

Reversed Polarity

Outlets with hot and neutral wires reversed, creating safety issues for appliances and potential shock hazards.

Fix: Rewire the outlet with correct polarity (requires an electrician to ensure proper connections).

Loose Outlets

Outlets that move or feel loose when plugging in devices can create loose wire connections and potential fire hazards.

Fix: Secure outlets properly to electrical boxes and replace damaged outlets.

Wiring & Connection Issues

Hidden wiring problems that home inspectors often discover and that should be addressed for safety.

Open Junction Boxes

Junction boxes missing covers expose wire connections, creating fire and shock hazards. These are often found in attics, crawl spaces, and basements.

Fix: Install proper covers on all junction boxes to protect wire connections.

Improper Wire Splices

Wires connected outside of junction boxes or with tape instead of proper wire nuts, creating serious fire hazards.

Fix: All wire connections must be made within approved junction boxes using proper connectors.

Improperly Secured Cables

Electrical cables hanging loosely or not properly secured to framing within 12 inches of boxes and every 4.5 feet thereafter.

Fix: Properly secure cables with approved staples or fasteners at required intervals.

Missing Wire Clamps

Cables entering boxes without proper clamps or bushings can lead to wire damage from sharp edges and potential shorts.

Fix: Install appropriate cable clamps or bushings where wires enter metal boxes.

Top Electrical Issues in Home Inspection Reports

These common findings often appear on inspection reports and may need to be addressed before selling your home.

1

Missing GFCI Protection in Wet Areas

Standard outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, basements, garages, and outdoor areas that lack GFCI protection are commonly flagged. GFCI outlets prevent electrical shock in areas where water may be present.

2

Outdated Electrical Panels

Federal Pacific, Zinsco, Sylvania, and fuse boxes are frequently cited in inspection reports due to known safety issues. These outdated panels don't provide adequate protection against electrical fires.

3

Double-Tapped Circuit Breakers

Multiple wires connected to a single breaker terminal creates loose connections and potential fire hazards. Inspectors consistently flag this issue as it's a clear code violation.

4

Open Junction Boxes

Missing covers on junction boxes expose wire connections, creating shock and fire hazards. These are often found in attics, basements, and crawl spaces during inspections.

5

Insufficient Outlets

Extensive use of power strips and extension cords due to lack of outlets is a safety concern. Modern code requires outlets every 12 feet along walls in living spaces.

6

Improperly Grounded Outlets

Three-prong outlets without proper grounding connections are regularly flagged in older homes. These create safety hazards for modern appliances requiring ground connections.

Pre-Sale Electrical Checklist

Address these issues before listing your home to avoid inspection surprises and negotiation headaches.

Electrical Panel

  • Replace outdated Federal Pacific, Zinsco, or fuse panels
  • Fix double-tapped breakers (multiple wires on one terminal)
  • Install missing knockout plugs/blanking plates
  • Properly label all circuit breakers
  • Ensure panel is accessible (not blocked by storage)

Outlets & Switches

  • Install GFCI outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, basements, and outdoors
  • Replace broken or cracked outlet/switch covers
  • Fix loose outlets and switches
  • Address ungrounded three-prong outlets
  • Replace painted-over outlets

Wiring Issues

  • Install covers on all junction boxes
  • Secure loose or hanging cables
  • Fix exposed wiring in accessible areas
  • Repair improper wire connections outside of junction boxes
  • Address extension cords used as permanent wiring

Safety Devices

  • Install smoke detectors in all required locations
  • Install carbon monoxide detectors if you have gas appliances
  • Ensure bathroom exhaust fans vent properly to outside
  • Verify proper clearance around heating appliances
  • Replace damaged or fraying power cords

Understanding GFCI & AFCI Requirements

These critical safety devices are often flagged in home inspections and vary by local jurisdiction.

GFCI Protection

Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters detect current leakage (like when electricity contacts water) and quickly shut off power to prevent shock.

Common GFCI Inspection Issues:

  • Missing GFCI protection in kitchens, especially for countertop outlets
  • Standard outlets in bathrooms without GFCI protection
  • Outdoor outlets lacking GFCI protection
  • Unprotected outlets in garages and unfinished basements

Note: GFCI protection can be provided either by GFCI outlets or by GFCI circuit breakers at the panel that protect all outlets on that circuit.

AFCI Protection

AFCI requirements vary significantly by jurisdiction and enforcement practices. Contact your local building department for specific requirements in your area.
