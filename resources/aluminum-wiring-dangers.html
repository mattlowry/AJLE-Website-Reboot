Important Information About Aluminum Wiring

Aluminum wiring was commonly used in homes built between 1965-1973.

While aluminum wiring itself isn't inherently unsafe, it has different properties than copper that can lead to connection issues over time, making professional evaluation and potential replacement a practical consideration for property owners.

Understanding Aluminum Wiring Properties

Learn about the unique characteristics of aluminum wiring and why professional evaluation is recommended.

Thermal Expansion

Aluminum has a higher rate of thermal expansion than copper, which can lead to connection loosening over decades of use.

Oxidation Properties

Aluminum naturally oxidizes when exposed to air, which can affect conductivity at connection points over time.

Connection Considerations

Proper connections and regular professional evaluation help ensure aluminum wiring continues to function safely and reliably.

Incompatible Devices

Many electrical devices were designed for copper and create galvanic corrosion when connected to aluminum.

Installation Issues

Aluminum requires special handling and connection methods that were not widely understood in the 1960s-70s.

Age-Related Failure

After 50+ years, aluminum wiring connections have had decades to deteriorate and become dangerous.

Is Your Home at Risk?

Aluminum wiring was primarily installed during a specific time period when copper prices were high.

1965-1973

HIGH RISK

Homes built during this period likely have aluminum branch circuit wiring throughout.

Action Required: Professional inspection immediately
1974-1976

MODERATE RISK

Some homes may have aluminum wiring, especially in certain regions or budget construction.

Recommended: Professional verification
1977+

LOW RISK

Copper wiring became standard again. Aluminum use was largely discontinued for branch circuits.

Status: Likely copper wiring
